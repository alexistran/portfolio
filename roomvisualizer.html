Floor & Decor

Room Visualizer


Room Visualizer is a new app for Floor & Decor that gives homeowners and professional contractors the ability to view and customize different surfaces and products inside an actual room. This allows customers to visualize their perfect home surfaces, which leads to more confident buying decisions.


Summer 2019

Role: Lead Product Designer

Team: Product Manager, 2 Developers, 4 Production Artists, Designer (myself)

Tools: Sketch, Adobe Illustrator, Anima, Invision, Draw.io, Google Docs





OVERVIEW

What's the Problem?

With many different products to offer, there was no way for users to visualize the products in a real space to make more informed buying decisions. This could potentially result in issues, such as more returns, as customers don’t know how the materials they select would look in an actual living space. We wanted the customers to be confident in their purchases.

Goals:

  • Create a Visualizer that is not just usable for desktop, but mobile friendly as well.
  • Ensure the Visualizer looks and feels consistent with the client’s brand.
  • Create an experience that shows off their products, but doesn’t overwhelm the user.


Contributions:

As the lead designer, I:
  • Collaborated with PM and engineers on our team as well as on Floor & Decor's team
  • Conducted UX discovery phase (Competitive Analysis, Stakeholder Meetings)
  • Designed user flows, wireframes, and prototypes
  • Designed iconography and other visuals needed
  • Led usability testing sessions
  • Presented weekly demos on design progress with the client
  • Created UI documentation for engineers





DISCOVERY

Identifying Problems through Market Research

Due to the time crunch, the research I was able to do was quite limited. Since the key features for our MVP were determined by the product managers on our side and on their side, I focused on understanding the issues and inspiration I can draw from other visualization applications, in particular, their layout and organizational structure. These competitors were selected by doing a quick online search on "flooring visualizer" and selecting the top results.



Key Findings

On Mobile

Most other visualizations apps did not consider the mobile experience at all. They were usually desktop versions that were smushed into a phone screen with a lot of overlapping text and images, making it unusable. We found an opportunity here to create a mobile responsive application that could shine on its own.

On How to Change Surface Options

Some visualization apps didn’t allow users to view the surface changing— the customization options and room visualization were not visible at the same time. This creates a tedious experience of having to switch back and forth between changing the surface and looking at the surface.

On Clarity

In an effort to fit more content into the screen without making it overwhelming, some apps used just iconography, but most of the time these icons were not clear. Iconography should reflect what the action is, or at least incorporate text to make it clearer.



User Flow

As this application was quite complex, I created a user flow to understand all the actions and paths a user could potentially take.

THE SOLUTION

Let's jump right into it


Design Decisions
Right when the user enters the Room Visualizer, there are no barriers— everything is ready to go. On desktop, the user can immediately start selecting the surface they want to modify and make selections in the right panel. On mobile, clicking on a surface will immediately open up the customization panel. The selected surface is outlined in an obvious green color to make it very clear which one is selected. All of the user's selections live underneath the room visualization, giving the user quick access to purchase any of their selections.



Customizing your room






Design Decisions:
All of the customization options live on the right. Users have the option to filter, sort, and search through products, making it easier to find exactly what they want.



Compare your products


Design Decisions:
When it comes to selecting materials for your home, this will usually be a long-term decision. Users are able to compare products side-by-side so that they can see what the difference is, no matter how miniscule. The "left" and "right" buttons make it super clear which side a selection is being made for.




TAKEAWAYS

What I Learned

  • A mobile-first design approach forces you to design for the device with the most constraints first, and then expand from there. It can be more difficult designing a bunch of features for desktops, and then figuring out how to reduce it for mobile.
  • Making user flows can be tedious, especially for more complex products, but it'll pay off later to have the flow defined and clear before jumping into designing.
  • What a client wants can hold priority over everything, even if it's not the best experience. The most you can do is explain your reasoning and the research to back it up, but ultimately it’s their decision.
  • Communication and organization are so important on large projects, especially when so many people are involved.
  • I learned new strategies in explaining designs to clients. I found it was helpful to have real life examples they can reference when I used a design pattern that was not immediately clear to them due to a static mockup.
  • I learned how to better phrase questions when conducting usability tests and the importance of how the words I use can bias their decisions.
