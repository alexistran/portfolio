Med Reminder


Aiding in the medication taking experience.

OVERVIEW

For individuals who have a medication regimen, one of the biggest challenges is being consistent with taking their medication, making sure that they remember to take it on time. Whether it’s because there’s too many medications to track, or simply forgetfulness, this is an issue because the medication is often crucial to the individual’s well-being. There needs to be a solution that can be seamlessly integrated into people's daily lives such that the problem of irregular medication taking is eliminated.

For the final project in my human centered design course, I worked to create a solution that would make remembering to take medication be an easier experience for people.

EXPLORING THE SPACE

USER RESEARCH: IN-PERSON INTERVIEWS

I conducted user interviews with 12 people. These people include those who currently take some form of medication, or have in the past, and people who work in healthcare.

Interview guide for people who have taken medication:

  1. Do you already regularly take medication? If so, how do you currently keep track of when you have to take them?
  2. Where do you store your medication?
  3. What time do you usually take your medication and how often?
  4. What's your morning/afternoon/evening routine generally like?
  5. If you take both essential and less essential medicine (like vitamins), are you more prone to forget to take less essential medicine, compared to medication for more life threatening issues?
  6. What different methods in the past have you tried in the past to help you remember?


SECONDARY RESEARCH

I conducted some additional secondary research, exploring other attempts to solve this problem.​​​​​​​


Physical pillbox


    Pros

  • stores your medication
  • everything easily separated by the day you need to take it

    • Cons

  • does not distinguish what time you need to take your medication

Basic reminder app


    Pros

  • easy to use to get a simple reminder
  • already comes downloaded with phone

    • Cons

  • does not let you categorize your medication in an organized manner

Medisafe


    Pros

  • current most popular medicine reminder app in the app store
  • many useful functions

    • Cons

  • a lot of functions that might be overwhelming if your main goal is to simplify the medication taking experience
  • layout of pills is ambiguous, which can cause confusion

Through conducting secondary research, and seeing users use these products, I was able to validate a need for a solution to the original problem. While there are existing products that attempt to aid the medication taking experience, they either did not do enough, or had too much functionality that it was an overwhelming experience. My goal is to create a solution with a balance between both. ​​​​​​​

  • you can casually carry around medication, but not toothpaste

  • not feasible for me to pursue

Medicine reminder app

  • most people always have their smartphone on them, which may make the addition of a reminder app not too much of a hassle
  • you can casually carry around medication, but not toothpaste
  • with time constraints, this is a better path to pursue
  • can work for all medications

THE SOLUTION

Med Reminder is a mobile app that simplifies and aids the medication taking experience. By using a device that people regularly use, we can tackle irregular medication use. This app is an effective reminder that people can conveniently incorporate into their daily busy lives. It not only provides medication reminders, but also streamlines other aspects of taking medication that may contribute to one not being able to take a medication on time. The design goal is to be simplistic and purposeful. In addition to the app, a push notification will be sent at the appropriate times to remind you to take your medication.

TAKEAWAYS

Work within constraints.

As this was the final project for my human centered design course, there were time limitations. If I had more time, I would like to conduct more user interviews. I'd like to continue iterating on the final prototype, to see how it could be further useful to people-- specifically interviewing more older generations because they are the ones taking the most medication but may not be the most tech savvy. Knowing my constraints helped me think about what I could realistically achieve, but also think beyond and ideate on how to make the impossible possible.


Improve through iterations.

The final solution can take many different tries to get to. Having a perfect solution right off the bat is highly unlikely, and getting feedback helped a lot in getting to where I am, which isn't perfect, but it's the process that matters. I had to make design decisions to find a middle ground between convenience and several potentially helpful features, which is a decision I didn't even know I had to make when I started. Iterating helps me reach the small details to create a delightful user experience. In addition, through later usability tests on my final prototype, I was able to gain a perspective I had not realized before. Privacy was a concern that did not occur to me as I was designing the app. Moving forward, I want to figure out how to incorporate more privacy into my apps, such as adding a pin code or password to gain access to the list of medications.


What is success?

A key performance indicator that I would use to measure the success of this app is if the amount of missed medications is significantly reduced from before they started using the app. ​​​​
